\subsection{Validation Network}

We validate our sampling model on the Greater London Authority (GLA) road network, sourced from Ordnance Survey Open Roads data. After preprocessing (removing filler nodes, consolidating nearby nodes, removing dangles), the network comprises approximately \glaNnodes{} nodes.

To eliminate edge artifacts, we apply a \glaBuffer{}km inward buffer from the convex hull of all nodes. This ensures that all ``live'' nodes included in accuracy calculations have complete catchments at all tested distances (up to 20km).

The GLA network is ideal for validation because:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Its scale (\glaNnodes{} nodes) far exceeds our synthetic training data
\item It contains diverse topology: grid-like central areas, tree-like suburbs, and mixed intermediate zones
\item Reach values at tested distances (5km, 10km, 20km) extend well beyond synthetic data ranges
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Experimental Protocol}

For each distance threshold, we:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Compute ground-truth centrality (full computation, $p = 1.0$)
\item Compute sampled centrality at multiple probabilities (1\%, 2\%, 5\%, 10\%, 15\%, 20\%, 30\%, 50\%, 75\%, 100\%)
\item Measure Spearman correlation $\rho$ between sampled and ground-truth values
\item Repeat with 3 random seeds and report mean $\pm$ standard deviation
\end{enumerate}

We compare observed accuracy against model predictions at the model-recommended sampling probability.

\subsection{Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:validation} shows validation results. Panel A plots observed accuracy ($\rho$) against sampling probability for each distance threshold, with the model-recommended probability marked. At all three distances, the model recommendation achieves $\rho > \glaMinRho$, exceeding the \targetRho{} target.

Panel B compares model predictions with observed outcomes. At the recommended sampling probability, observed $\rho$ consistently exceeds model predictions, demonstrating that the model is appropriately conservative.

\subsection{Extrapolation Performance}

The validation demonstrates successful extrapolation beyond synthetic training data:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Network scale}: Training used networks of $\approx 2000$ nodes; validation uses \glaNnodes{} nodes
\item \textbf{Reach range}: Training reached up to $\approx 2600$; validation reaches \glaTwentyKmReach{} (16$\times$ higher)
\item \textbf{Topology diversity}: Training used controlled synthetic topologies; validation uses real-world heterogeneous structure
\end{itemize}

Despite these extrapolations, the model's predictions remain conservative. This suggests that the $\effn$-accuracy relationship identified in synthetic data is a robust property of network centrality, not an artifact of specific network configurations.

\subsection{Variance Across Random Seeds}

Sampling introduces variance: different random seeds produce slightly different centrality estimates. Table~\ref{tab:validation} includes standard deviations across 3 replications.

At recommended sampling probabilities, standard deviations are small ($< 0.005$ in $\rho$). This indicates that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A single sampled computation is sufficient for most purposes
\item When higher precision is needed, averaging 2--3 replications provides excellent stability
\end{enumerate}

The low variance arises because our floor constraint ensures $\effn \geq \minEffN$, guaranteeing a minimum sample size that provides statistical stability.
