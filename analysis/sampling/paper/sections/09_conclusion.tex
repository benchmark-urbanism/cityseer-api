% Section 9: Conclusion
% =============================================================================

We have presented a two-parameter model that determines the sampling probability for network centrality approximation from network reach alone. Formal testing confirms the square root scaling assumption, and model comparison across five candidate functional forms supports the proposed specification. Validation on the Greater London (\glaNnodes{} nodes) and Madrid street networks achieves Spearman $\rho > \glaMinRho$ at all tested distances (5--20km), with speedups of \glaFiveKmSpeedup{}--\glaTwentyKmSpeedup{}$\times$---demonstrating that the model extrapolates well beyond the synthetic networks on which it was trained.

The practical value lies in providing analysts with a calibrated way to trade computation time for approximation error, with confidence that sampled results preserve ranking accuracy. The largest benefits accrue at long distances on large networks---precisely where computational cost is highest. More broadly, our results suggest that the effective sample size--accuracy relationship is a consistent property of real-world network centrality rather than an artifact of specific configurations, though further validation on non-European cities would strengthen this claim.

The model is implemented in the open-source \cityseer{} Python library.\footnote{\texttt{https://github.com/benchmark-urbanism/cityseer-api}} Fitting scripts, validation data, and model comparison analyses are available in the repository's \texttt{analysis/sampling/} directory.
