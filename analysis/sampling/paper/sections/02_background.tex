\subsection{Computational Cost of Network Centrality}

Standard algorithms for localised centrality are built on repeated single-source shortest path computations, but the precise complexity depends on the metric and whether edges are weighted. For distance-based (weighted) closeness variants, one typically runs Dijkstra from every source: each run is $O(M \log N)$ with a binary heap, yielding $O(NM \log N)$ overall. For betweenness, Brandes' algorithm also performs a shortest-path computation from each source followed by a dependency-accumulation pass; the classic bounds are $O(NM)$ for unweighted graphs (BFS per source) and $O(NM + N^2 \log N)$ for weighted graphs \citep{Brandes2001}. In sparse street graphs where $M=O(N)$, these costs are commonly superlinear and often effectively quadratic in practice.

The practical consequence is straightforward. A network of \glaNnodes{} nodes requires \glaNnodes{} Dijkstra runs. Even with distance cutoffs that limit individual traversals, the per-source cost grows with the analysis distance: at a 20\,km threshold in a dense urban network, each run may visit tens of thousands of nodes. A single centrality computation at one distance can take hours. Urban morphology studies routinely require multiple distance thresholds, typically spanning two orders of magnitude from pedestrian to metropolitan scales, multiplying the total cost accordingly.

\subsection{Sampling-Based Approximation}

Both closeness and betweenness can be expressed as sums over contributions from individual source nodes: each source launches a shortest-path computation that contributes to the centrality of every node it reaches. This additive structure makes sampling straightforward --- rather than computing from every source, one can compute from a random subset and rescale to estimate the full sum. Under uniform source sampling, Horvitz--Thompson-style rescaling (dividing by the inclusion probability) yields an unbiased estimator of the population sum; ratio estimators such as H\'{a}jek are generally consistent and often lower-variance but can be slightly biased in finite samples \citep{Cochran1977, Sarndal2003}. In either case, sampling error decreases at an approximate $1/\sqrt{k}$ rate with the number of sampled sources, up to finite-population corrections.

\citet{Brandes2007} introduced this idea for both closeness and betweenness, showing that random pivot selection from a small fraction of source nodes produces useful centrality estimates on networks of tens of thousands of nodes. However, their work did not provide formal error bounds or guidance on choosing the number of sources.

\citet{Eppstein2004} gave the first formal bound for source-sampled closeness centrality, showing that $O(\log n / \varepsilon^2)$ single-source shortest path computations suffice to approximate the closeness of all vertices to within additive error $\varepsilon \cdot \Delta_G$ with high probability, where $\Delta_G$ is the graph diameter. Their approach (sampling source nodes uniformly and averaging the resulting distance estimates) is the most directly comparable to ours, as both methods reduce computation by selecting a random subset of sources. The $\log n$ factor in their bound depends on the total number of nodes, regardless of the analysis distance.

\citet{Bader2007} gave the first probabilistic error bound for sampled betweenness, showing that $O(1/\varepsilon^2)$ samples suffice to approximate the betweenness of a single vertex to within additive error $\varepsilon$ with high probability. Their adaptive algorithm doubles the sample count until the confidence interval contracts below a user-specified threshold. The bound applies per vertex and does not directly provide uniform approximation across all vertices simultaneously.

\citet{Riondato2016} addressed the uniform approximation problem using Vapnik--Chervonenkis (VC) dimension theory \citep{Vapnik1971}. They showed that the number of samples required for a uniform additive $\varepsilon$-approximation of all betweenness values depends on the VC-dimension of the set of shortest paths, which is bounded by $\lfloor \log_2 (\text{VD} - 2) \rfloor + 1$, where VD is the vertex-diameter of the graph. This yields sample complexity logarithmic in the vertex-diameter rather than the number of nodes. The resulting bounds are worst-case, however, and can be conservative for well-structured networks such as urban street grids.

For closeness centrality specifically, \citet{Cohen2014} proposed an approximation framework that exploits the structure of closeness (a sum of inverse distances) to achieve tighter bounds than generic betweenness methods. Their approach uses random sampling of target nodes rather than source nodes and provides per-vertex confidence intervals.

\citet{Borassi2019} introduced KADABRA, an adaptive algorithm for betweenness that adjusts the number of samples online. KADABRA maintains running confidence intervals for each vertex's betweenness and terminates when all intervals are sufficiently narrow, eliminating the need to specify the number of samples in advance. \citet{Bergamini2019} provided a comparative evaluation of these and related approaches, finding that adaptive methods generally outperform fixed-sample methods in terms of accuracy per sample, though the overhead of online monitoring can offset some of the savings.

Most recently, \citet{Pellegrina2023} introduced SILVAN, which replaces VC-dimension bounds with Monte Carlo Rademacher averages to obtain tighter, \emph{variance-dependent} sample complexity bounds for betweenness. Because SILVAN's bounds depend on the actual variance of the betweenness estimator rather than worst-case combinatorial quantities, they can be substantially tighter on well-structured graphs, an observation consistent with our empirical finding that urban street networks require far fewer samples than worst-case theory predicts. However, SILVAN still targets global betweenness with additive $\varepsilon$-guarantees.

These methods divide into two fundamentally different sampling strategies. \emph{Source-sampling} (Brandes, Eppstein--Wang, Bader) selects a random subset of source nodes and runs a full shortest-path tree from each; every node reachable from a sampled source receives a centrality contribution. \emph{Path-sampling} (Riondato--Kornaropoulos, KADABRA, SILVAN) instead draws random source--target pairs and computes a single shortest path between them, counting betweenness contributions along that path. Cohen's approach is a third variant, \emph{target-sampling}, which samples destination nodes and propagates distance information backward. The distinction matters because standard centrality algorithms, including localised implementations with distance cutoffs, are inherently source-based: each source launches a Dijkstra traversal that is naturally bounded by the distance threshold $d_{\max}$, producing contributions to every node within reach. Source-sampling bounds therefore analyse the same computational primitive that localised centrality uses, whereas path-sampling bounds analyse a different primitive whose relationship to distance-bounded computation is less straightforward.

A common feature of this body of work is that it targets \emph{global} centrality, computed over the entire graph without distance cutoffs, on general (non-spatial) networks. Urban network analysis, by contrast, uses \emph{localised} centrality with distance thresholds that range from hundreds of metres to tens of kilometres. The reachable subgraph at each threshold varies by orders of magnitude, from hundreds of nodes at pedestrian scales to tens of thousands at metropolitan scales. The theoretical bounds reviewed above, expressed in terms of graph diameter, VC-dimension, or total node count, do not directly account for this distance-dependent structure. Whether a single model can bridge this gap, determining sample size from reachability alone across multiple distance scales, is the question we address.
