\subsection{Network Centrality in Urban Analysis}

Network centrality measures have become essential tools for urban morphology and spatial analysis. Originally developed in social network analysis \citep{Freeman1977, Freeman1979}, these measures were adapted for urban street networks by \citet{Hillier1984} and subsequently formalised in the space syntax tradition.

\textbf{Closeness centrality} measures how efficiently a node can reach all others. The harmonic variant, which we use throughout this paper, is defined as:
\begin{equation}
C_H(v) = \sum_{u \neq v} \frac{1}{d(v, u)}
\end{equation}
where $d(v, u)$ is the shortest path distance from $v$ to $u$. The harmonic formulation handles disconnected graphs naturally and is preferred for distance-weighted urban analysis \citep{Porta2006}.

\textbf{Betweenness centrality} measures the extent to which a node lies on shortest paths between other pairs:
\begin{equation}
C_B(v) = \sum_{s \neq v \neq t} \frac{\sigma_{st}(v)}{\sigma_{st}}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{st}$ is the number of shortest paths from $s$ to $t$, and $\sigma_{st}(v)$ is the number passing through $v$. High betweenness indicates locations that control flow through the network.

For urban applications, these measures are typically computed with a distance threshold $d_{\max}$, considering only nodes within a specified radius. This reflects the reality that pedestrian accessibility is local rather than global. Common thresholds range from 400m (5-minute walk) to 20km (regional connectivity).

\subsection{Computational Complexity}

The computational cost of centrality measures scales poorly with network size. Brandes' algorithm \citep{Brandes2001} computes both betweenness and harmonic closeness in $O(NM + N^2 \log N)$ time for weighted graphs, where $N$ is nodes and $M$ is edges. For a network of \glaNnodes{} nodes with average degree 3, this translates to billions of operations per distance threshold.

When analysis requires multiple distance thresholds---as is common in urban morphology studies---the computational burden multiplies. A typical multi-scale analysis with 12 distance thresholds on a large network can require days of computation.

\subsection{Sampling Approximation}

Sampling-based approximation of centrality has been studied extensively. \citet{Brandes2007} showed that betweenness can be approximated by computing from a random subset of source nodes and scaling appropriately. Subsequent work established theoretical bounds on approximation quality \citep{Bader2007, Riondato2016}.

The key insight is that centrality computed from $k$ random sources produces estimates with standard error proportional to $1/\sqrt{k}$. This suggests that effective sample size---the product of reach and sampling probability---should be the key determinant of accuracy.

However, prior work has focused on theoretical worst-case bounds rather than empirical calibration for practical use. The question of \emph{how many samples are sufficient for urban network analysis} has not been systematically addressed. Our work fills this gap by:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Empirically validating that effective sample size determines accuracy across topologies
\item Fitting practical thresholds for achieving target accuracy ($\rho \geq \targetRho$)
\item Deriving a simple model that practitioners can apply directly
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Distance-Weighted Centrality}

Urban network analysis typically uses \emph{local} centrality measures with distance thresholds. For a threshold $d_{\max}$, we compute:
\begin{equation}
C_H(v; d_{\max}) = \sum_{u: d(v,u) \leq d_{\max}} \frac{1}{d(v, u)}
\end{equation}

The \emph{reach} $r$ at distance $d_{\max}$ is the mean number of nodes within that distance:
\begin{equation}
r = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{v} \left| \{u : d(v, u) \leq d_{\max}\} \right|
\end{equation}

Reach varies dramatically with distance threshold. In our Greater London validation network, reach ranges from \glaFiveKmReach{} at 5km to \glaTwentyKmReach{} at 20km---a 15-fold increase. This variation is central to our sampling model: larger reach allows more aggressive sampling while maintaining accuracy.
