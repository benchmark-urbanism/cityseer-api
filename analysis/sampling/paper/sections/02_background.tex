% Background and Related Work section

Network centrality measures are widely used in urban analysis, with modern tools such as OSMnx \citep{Boeing2017}, sDNA \citep{Cooper2018}, and \cityseer{} \citep{Simons2022} enabling large-scale computation on street networks. For pedestrian-scale analysis, \emph{localised} centrality measures restrict computation to nodes within a distance threshold \citep{Turner2007,Cooper2015}, reflecting that accessibility patterns relevant to walking behaviour operate at limited distances. Multi-scale analysis---computing centrality at multiple thresholds (e.g., 500m, 1000m, 2000m, 5000m)---has become standard practice for capturing both neighbourhood accessibility and broader urban connectivity.

The \citet{Brandes2001} algorithm provides the foundation for efficient centrality computation, yet multi-scale localised analysis on large urban networks remains prohibitive for rapid planning iteration. Sampling-based approximation \citep{Brandes2007} estimates centrality from a subset of source nodes, with error decreasing as $1/\sqrt{k}$ samples. However, prior work has focused primarily on global rather than localised measures, leaving a gap in understanding how sampling performs across multiple distance thresholds.

The specific challenge of multi-scale localised centrality has received little attention. At short distances, urban networks have low reachability (few nodes within threshold), while at long distances, reachability approaches the full network. Uniform sampling fails to address this variation: a fixed sampling probability that works at long distances provides insufficient samples at short distances. This paper addresses this gap by introducing \emph{effective sample size} as a unifying concept and developing an adaptive algorithm that calibrates sampling probability independently for each distance threshold.
