\subsection{Practical Guidelines and Limitations}

Adaptive sampling provides the greatest advantage for multi-scale analyses on large networks ($>$10,000 nodes), where uniform sampling cannot maintain consistent accuracy across distance thresholds. Alternative approaches are preferred when: single-distance or long-distance-only analyses benefit adequately from uniform sampling; small networks ($<$500 nodes) complete full computation in milliseconds; very short distances ($<$300m) have inherently low reachability; or regulatory requirements necessitate exact values.

Practitioners should review the logged sampling plan to verify expected accuracy for each distance, and when computing both metrics, use the more conservative model for the chosen distance type: harmonic closeness for shortest paths, betweenness for angular paths. Setting the target slightly higher than needed (e.g., $\rhosp^* = 0.96$ when 0.95 suffices) provides additional robustness.

Several limitations should be noted. The empirical models were fitted on specific network topologies; networks with unusual characteristics (extreme sparsity, very long edges) may require recalibration. At very low $\effn$ (< 25), observed accuracy shows high variance and the algorithm defaults to full computation when required $p > 1.0$. While the paper focuses on ranking accuracy, magnitude accuracy is lower and more variable---applications requiring accurate absolute values should use higher sampling rates. Adaptive sampling runs separate Dijkstra computations per distance threshold, so analyses with many thresholds may see reduced speedup compared to single-pass full computation. Spatial heterogeneity in harmonic closeness accuracy is documented in Appendix~C. The power correction model adds a penalty term $C \times (1-p)^D$ that accounts for additional variance at low sampling probabilities; this correction is most important for betweenness centrality where the residual-$p$ correlation is reduced from approximately 0.3 to near zero.

Future research could derive the model form from first principles via central limit theorem arguments, extend the approach to other localised metrics such as gravity-weighted accessibility, or explore topology-aware models incorporating local features to better predict accuracy in transitional zones.
