\subsection{The Opportunity: Sampling Can Work}

Figure~\ref{fig:headline} illustrates why sampling-based centrality is attractive. Panel~A shows that accuracy (Spearman $\rho$) increases with sampling probability across all distance thresholds, but the curves flatten well before 100\% sampling. At 4000m distance, $\rho \geq \targetRho$ is achieved at just 10\% sampling, a 10$\times$ potential speedup. Panel~B shows that, for a given level of accuracy, the relative speedup from reduced sampling becomes more pronounced as the analysis distance grows. The remainder of this section develops a model calibrated to the rank-preservation target and evaluates whether the resulting sample sizes also provide formal additive-error guarantees.

\subsection{Effective Sample Size and Proportional Scaling}

We define the \emph{effective sample size} $\effn = r \times p$, where $r$ is the mean reachability (average number of nodes reachable per source within the distance threshold) and $p$ is the sampling probability. Figure~\ref{fig:derivation}A shows the relationship between $\effn$ and accuracy (Spearman $\rho$): accuracy improves with $\effn$, but the required $\effn$ is not constant; it grows with reach.

Figure~\ref{fig:derivation}B quantifies this: the minimum $\effn$ required to achieve $\rho \geq \targetRho$ scales as $\effn \propto \sqrt{r}$, which we parameterise as:
\begin{equation}
  \effn = k \cdot \sqrt{r}
\end{equation}

This scaling can be motivated from survey sampling, but it should be read as a variance-scaling heuristic rather than a universal theorem: the exact relationship depends on how per-source contributions co-vary on real networks and on how ranking stability relates to estimation noise. When $n$ source nodes are sampled uniformly from $N$ live nodes, the H\'{a}jek estimator \citep{Hajek1971} for the centrality of node $v$ has variance
\begin{equation}
  \text{Var}(\hat{C}(v)) = \left(\frac{1}{p} - 1\right) \sigma^2_v
  \label{eq:hajek_var}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma^2_v$ is the variance of per-source contributions to $C(v)$ and $p = n/N$ is the sampling probability \citep{Cochran1977, Sarndal2003}. For summation-based centrality measures (both harmonic closeness and betweenness), the centrality of $v$ can be written as a sum over source contributions within the distance threshold. As reach $r$ grows, the number of contributing terms grows and the variance of the (finite-population) estimand typically increases with $r$; under an idealised picture in which contributions are weakly dependent and have comparable scale across reachable sources, it is reasonable to expect $\sigma^2_v$ to grow approximately linearly in $r$.

If we further assume that ranking stability is controlled primarily by a roughly constant coefficient of variation $\text{CV} = \sigma_v / C(v)$ across distances, then to keep CV approximately constant as $r$ increases we need the sampling-induced variance inflation factor $\left(\tfrac{1}{p} - 1\right)$ to shrink accordingly. This leads to an effective sample size requirement that grows sublinearly with reach, and in the linear-variance idealisation above yields $n \propto \sqrt{r}$, i.e.\ $\effn = k \cdot \sqrt{r}$ for some constant $k$. We treat this as a theoretically motivated hypothesis and test it empirically below.

To validate this prediction empirically, we fit the general power model $\effn = k \cdot r^{\alpha}$ via OLS on log-transformed data ($R^2 = \alphaRSquared$). The estimated exponent $\hat{\alpha} = \alphaHat$ (bootstrap 95\% CI: $[\alphaCILower, \alphaCIUpper]$) does not differ significantly from 0.5 ($p = \alphaTestPValue$), confirming the theoretical prediction (Figure~\ref{fig:power_diagnostics}).

For each synthetic configuration that achieves $\rho \geq \targetRho$, we compute the implied $k = p \cdot \sqrt{r}$. We take the 75th percentile across all \nConfigs{} configurations rather than the mean or median: this ensures that the model is conservative, providing sufficient samples for the majority of topology--distance combinations rather than only the average case. This yields $k = \kProp$.

\subsection{The Floor: Reliability at Intermediate Reach}

The proportional model $\effn = k \cdot \sqrt{r}$ does not provide reliable estimates at reach lower than approximately \crossoverReach. At very low reach ($r \lesssim k^2 \approx 100$), the model prescribes $p = 1.0$ (full computation), so the floor is moot. But at intermediate reach ($100 \lesssim r \lesssim \crossoverReach$), the model prescribes $p < 1$ with $\effn < \minEffN$, which our data indicate is insufficient for reliable estimates.

Figure~\ref{fig:floor} demonstrates the problem. Panel~A shows success rates (fraction of configurations achieving $\rho \geq \targetRho$) binned by effective sample size. Below $\effn = \minEffN$, success rates are erratic; above this threshold, they stabilise at 100\%.

Panel~B illustrates why: the proportional model's prescribed $\effn = k\sqrt{r}$ falls below $\minEffN$ throughout the intermediate reach range, creating a deficit (shaded region) where Panel~A shows reliability is poor. The two regimes meet at the crossover reach of $\approx \crossoverReach$; above this, the proportional model provides sufficient $\effn$ on its own.

We determine the floor more rigorously using logistic regression: fitting $P(\rho \geq \targetRho) = \text{logistic}(\beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \effn)$ and inverting to find the $\effn$ where predicted success probability reaches 95\%. This yields $n_{\min} = \floorLogistic$ (95\% CI: $[\floorCILower, \floorCIUpper]$), confirming the binned analysis while providing uncertainty quantification (Table~\ref{tab:parameters}).

\subsection{The Complete Model}

Combining the proportional scaling with the minimum floor yields our final model:
\begin{equation}
  \effn = \max\left( \kProp \cdot \sqrt{r}, \minEffN \right)
\end{equation}

The sampling probability follows directly:
\begin{equation}
  p = \min\left(1, \frac{\effn}{r}\right)
\end{equation}

Figure~\ref{fig:combined} shows the complete model. Panel A displays how the required $\effn$ transitions from the floor regime (flat at $\minEffN$) to the proportional regime (scaling as $\sqrt{r}$) at the crossover point $r = \crossoverReach$. Panel B shows the corresponding sampling probability, which decreases as $1/\sqrt{r}$ once past the crossover.

\subsection{Model Selection}

To justify the proposed $\sqrt{r}$ + floor specification against alternatives, we compare \nModelsCompared{} candidate models: plain $\sqrt{r}$, $\sqrt{r}$ + floor (proposed), general power $r^{\alpha}$, general power + floor, and logarithmic. Models are fitted by maximum likelihood using a logistic observation model and evaluated by AIC and leave-one-topology-out cross-validated Brier score (Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}).

The \bestModelName{} model ($\effn = k \cdot r^{\alpha}$, with $\hat{\alpha} = \alphaHat$) achieves the lowest AIC (\bestModelAIC), while the proposed $\sqrt{r}$ + floor model has $\Delta$AIC = \proposedDeltaAIC. However, cross-validated Brier scores are similar across all models (\bestModelBrier{} vs.\ \proposedModelBrier{}), indicating that differences in out-of-sample prediction are modest: the AIC advantage does not translate into meaningfully better generalisation to held-out topologies. We retain the $\sqrt{r}$ specification for three reasons: (1) the hypothesis test does not reject $\alpha = 0.5$ ($p = \alphaTestPValue$), so the data are consistent with square root scaling; (2) fixing $\alpha = 0.5$ provides a direct connection to sampling theory ($\text{SE} \propto 1/\sqrt{n}$), making the model interpretable rather than purely empirical; and (3) the floor component addresses a qualitatively distinct failure mode at low reach that none of the single power law candidates capture, even though the floor's contribution to in-sample fit is small. This is a modelling choice driven by interpretability and robustness rather than in-sample fit.

\subsection{Relationship to Theoretical Bounds}
\label{sec:bounds}

The existing literature on centrality approximation provides worst-case sample complexity bounds expressed in terms of additive error $\varepsilon$ (Section~\ref{sec:background}). Table~\ref{tab:bounds_comparison} summarises how these results relate to our approach.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Comparison of sampling approaches for centrality approximation. All prior methods target global centrality (no distance cutoff) on general graphs. Our model targets localised centrality on urban street networks.}
\label{tab:bounds_comparison}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lllll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Method} & \textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Error type} & \textbf{Sample complexity} & \textbf{Key parameter} \\
\midrule
Eppstein--Wang & Closeness & Additive $\varepsilon$ & $O(\log n \,/\, \varepsilon^2)$ & Node count $n$ \\
Bader et al.\ & Betweenness & Additive $\varepsilon$ & $O(1 \,/\, \varepsilon^2)$ & Per vertex \\
Riondato--Korn.\ & Betweenness & Uniform $\varepsilon$ & $O(\text{VD} \,/\, \varepsilon^2)$ & VC-dimension \\
KADABRA & Betweenness & Uniform $\varepsilon$ & $O(\log D \,/\, \varepsilon^2)$ & Diameter $D$ \\
SILVAN & Betweenness & Non-uniform $\varepsilon$ & Variance-dependent & Path variance \\
\textbf{This work} & \textbf{Both} & \textbf{Rank $\rho$} & $\boldsymbol{\max(k\sqrt{r},\, n_{\min})}$ & \textbf{Reach $r$} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Among the bounds surveyed here, Eppstein--Wang is the only one that analyses source-sampling, the same computational primitive our algorithm uses. The path-sampling bounds (Riondato--Kornaropoulos, KADABRA, SILVAN) are not straightforwardly adapted to the localised setting, and Bader et al.'s per-vertex bound does not directly provide simultaneous guarantees. We therefore adapt the Eppstein--Wang bound to localised centrality by replacing the global node count with the distance-bounded reach, yielding a localised bound of $O(\log r / \varepsilon^2)$.

Three factors explain why our model requires substantially fewer samples than these bounds predict. First, the relevant graph for each node is the local subgraph of $r$ reachable nodes, not the full network, and the effective sample size $\effn = p \cdot r$ depends on this local quantity. Second, our rank-correlation target ($\rho \geq \targetRho$) is strictly weaker than uniform additive $\varepsilon$-approximation: Spearman $\rho$ measures average rank agreement, not worst-case accuracy at every node, so the $\log n$ union-bound factor is not required. Third, urban street networks are planar with bounded degree, constraining estimator variance well below worst-case assumptions for general graphs.

At high reach, the two perspectives converge. The rank model's recommended effective sample size corresponds to an implied additive error of $\varepsilon(r) = \sqrt{\log(2r/\delta) / (2 k^2 r)}$, which decreases with reach: at $r \approx 3{,}000$, the implied $\varepsilon \approx 0.1$, and at $r = 30{,}000$ it is about 0.06. The localised bound holds in over 93\% of synthetic configurations, with violations confined to low reach where concentration guarantees are weak and sampling offers limited computational benefit (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_ew}). We report error bounds alongside rank accuracy on the validation networks in Section~\ref{sec:validation}.

\subsection{Adaptive Sampling Algorithm}

The model determines a sampling probability for each distance threshold based on reach. Because reach varies with distance, optimal sampling also varies: short distances (low reach) may require full computation, while long distances enable aggressive sampling. Algorithm~\ref{alg:adaptive} presents the complete procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
  \caption{Adaptive Per-Distance Sampling for Network Centrality}
  \label{alg:adaptive}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require Network $G = (V, E)$, distance thresholds $D = \{d_1, \ldots, d_m\}$
    \Require Model parameters $k = \kProp$, $n_{\min} = \minEffN$
    \Ensure Centrality estimates $\hat{C}[d]$ for each $d \in D$

    \State \textbf{Phase 1: Reach Estimation}
    \For{each distance $d \in D$}
    \State Compute reach $r_d \gets$ mean nodes within distance $d$
    \EndFor

    \State \textbf{Phase 2: Adaptive Sampling}
    \For{each distance $d \in D$}
    \State Compute effective sample size: $n_{\text{eff},d} \gets \max(k \cdot \sqrt{r_d}, n_{\min})$
    \State Compute sampling probability: $p_d \gets \min(1, n_{\text{eff},d} / r_d)$
    \State Sample sources $S_d \subseteq V$ with probability $p_d$
    \State Compute centrality from $S_d$: $\hat{C}[d] \gets \textsc{Centrality}(G, S_d, d)$
    \State Scale estimates: $\hat{C}[d] \gets \hat{C}[d] / p_d$
    \EndFor

    \State \Return $\{\hat{C}[d] : d \in D\}$
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The reach $r_d$ is typically a byproduct of the centrality computation itself; when a quick estimate is needed beforehand, a pilot sample of $\approx 100$ nodes distributed on a square kilometre grid suffices. Sources are sampled uniformly at random, and sampled values are scaled by $1/p$ to produce unbiased estimates. This scaling is exact for betweenness (each source contributes independently) and approximate for harmonic closeness, though our floor constraint ($\effn \geq \minEffN$) ensures $p$ is never low enough for the approximation to matter.

\subsection{Computational Complexity}

With sampling probability $p$, we compute from $pN$ sources instead of $N$, reducing single-source computations by a factor of $1/p$. The theoretical speedup at high reach is $\sqrt{r}/k$, since $p = k/\sqrt{r}$; larger networks benefit more from sampling. For a network with distances spanning 500\,m to 20\,km, the model prescribes $p = 1.0$ at 500\,m (reach $\approx 200$, no benefit), $p \approx 0.26$ at 5\,km (reach $\approx 3{,}000$), and $p \approx 0.07$ at 20\,km (reach $\approx 40{,}000$), yielding a theoretical $14\times$ speedup at the longest distance.
