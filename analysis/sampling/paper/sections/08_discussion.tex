\subsection{Practical Guidelines}

For users of the \cityseer{} library, the adaptive sampling model is applied automatically. For other frameworks, the procedure is: (1) estimate reach $r$ at each analysis distance, (2) compute $\effn = \max(\kProp \cdot \sqrt{r}, \minEffN)$ and $p = \min(1, \effn/r)$, and (3) compute centrality from a random $p$-fraction of sources, scaling results by $1/p$.

As a rough guide: at reach below $\crossoverReach$, use full computation, since sampling overhead outweighs savings. At reach 1,000--10,000, sample 10--30\% (3--10$\times$ speedup). At reach above 10,000, sample 5--15\% (7--20$\times$ speedup). For applications requiring higher accuracy than $\rho \geq \targetRho$, increase $\effn$ by a factor of 1.5--2$\times$ or average 2--3 replications with different random seeds.

Sampling is not appropriate when exact centrality values (not approximations) are required, when centrality is needed for specific nodes rather than the full network, or when the network is small enough ($< 1{,}000$ nodes) that full computation is already fast.

\subsection{Model Assumptions and Limitations}

Our model rests on several assumptions. First, we assume uniform random sampling of source nodes. Non-uniform strategies (stratified by location or weighted toward high-centrality sources) could potentially achieve higher accuracy with fewer samples, but would require information about node importance that is unavailable before computation. Second, the model treats the network as statistically homogeneous: accuracy depends on global reach, not local structure. Real networks have heterogeneous topology, with dense urban cores surrounded by sparse suburbs. Our validation on the GLA and Madrid networks, which exhibit such heterogeneity, shows the model remains conservative overall, but accuracy may vary locally. For applications requiring spatially uniform accuracy, local calibration or stratified sampling could be explored. Third, we fitted the model primarily to betweenness centrality, which is more demanding than harmonic closeness. The model is therefore conservative for closeness, where lower sampling probabilities would suffice. Finally, our experiments used shortest-path distance; angular (simplest-path) distance, common in space syntax analysis, may exhibit different accuracy characteristics and warrants systematic validation.

\subsection{Future Directions}
\label{sec:future}

Several extensions could improve this work. On the theoretical side, proving that $n_{\text{eff}} \propto \sqrt{r}$ suffices specifically for rank stability with high probability remains an open problem; such a result would require bounding how per-source centrality contributions scale with reach across different topologies. The conservatism of the Hoeffding-based bound (Section~\ref{sec:bounds}) suggests that tighter concentration inequalities exploiting graph structure could reduce the gap between theoretical and empirical sample requirements. SILVAN's variance-dependent bounds \citep{Pellegrina2023}, which replace worst-case combinatorial quantities with the actual estimator variance, represent a promising direction for tightening the localised bound on well-structured network classes.

On the practical side, adaptive per-node sampling, where the probability varies with local reach, could improve efficiency in heterogeneous networks. Importance sampling, weighting toward high-centrality sources, could reduce variance. Online stopping criteria, as in KADABRA \citep{Borassi2019}, could replace a priori calibration with adaptive termination based on observed convergence, but adapting such methods to localised, distance-bounded centrality with rank-correlation targets would require new theoretical development. Systematic validation for angular distance and extension to other centrality measures (eigenvector centrality, PageRank) would broaden applicability.
