\subsection{Model Assumptions and Limitations}

Our model rests on several assumptions. First, we assume uniform random sampling of source nodes. Non-uniform strategies---stratified by location or weighted toward high-centrality sources---could potentially achieve higher accuracy with fewer samples, but would require information about node importance that is unavailable before computation. Second, the model treats the network as statistically homogeneous: accuracy depends on global reach, not local structure. Real networks have heterogeneous topology, with dense urban cores surrounded by sparse suburbs. Our validation on the GLA and Madrid networks, which exhibit such heterogeneity, shows the model remains conservative overall, but accuracy may vary locally. For applications requiring spatially uniform accuracy, local calibration or stratified sampling could be explored. Third, we fitted the model primarily to betweenness centrality, which is more demanding than harmonic closeness. The model is therefore conservative for closeness, where lower sampling probabilities would suffice. Finally, our experiments used shortest-path distance; angular (simplest-path) distance, common in space syntax analysis, may exhibit different accuracy characteristics and warrants systematic validation.

\subsection{Comparison with Theoretical Bounds}

Theoretical work on centrality approximation \citep{Riondato2016} provides worst-case bounds on approximation error as a function of sample size. These bounds are typically conservative, requiring many more samples than our empirical analysis suggests are sufficient. The gap arises because theoretical bounds must hold for adversarial graph structures, while real urban networks have regular, well-behaved topology. Our approach trades worst-case guarantees for practical efficiency, achieving high accuracy with fewer samples by exploiting this regularity. For applications where worst-case bounds are required, theoretical approaches should be preferred; for typical urban analysis, our empirical model provides more practical guidance.

\subsection{Practical Considerations}

Sampling introduces computational overhead beyond the centrality computation itself: random source selection, reach estimation, and result scaling. For very short distances (low reach), this overhead may exceed the savings. Our floor constraint ($\effn \geq \minEffN$) mitigates this by preventing sampling when benefit is minimal, but analysts should be aware that the crossover point ($r \approx \crossoverReach$) represents the minimum reach where sampling provides meaningful speedup.

In multi-scale analysis, which computes centrality at many distance thresholds simultaneously, computational savings are uneven: short distances may dominate total runtime even when long distances are heavily sampled. Analysts should consider whether all distance thresholds are necessary, or whether interpolation from a subset of distances could provide adequate resolution.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Several extensions could improve this work. Adaptive per-node sampling, where the probability varies with local reach, could improve efficiency in heterogeneous networks. Importance sampling, weighting toward high-centrality sources, could reduce variance. Online calibration---adjusting sampling probability during computation based on observed variance---could provide tighter adaptation. Systematic validation for angular distance and extension to other centrality measures (eigenvector centrality, PageRank) would broaden applicability.
