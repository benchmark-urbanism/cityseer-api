% Section 1: Introduction
% =============================================================================

Network centrality measures are fundamental tools for understanding urban street networks, revealing patterns of accessibility, connectivity, and flow that shape how cities function. Harmonic closeness centrality quantifies how effectively a location can reach all others, while betweenness centrality captures the extent to which a location lies on shortest paths between other pairs of nodes. These measures have proven invaluable for urban planning, retail location analysis, and understanding pedestrian movement patterns.

However, computing network centrality at scale presents a significant computational challenge. Standard algorithms have time complexity $O(N \cdot M + N^2 \log N)$ for weighted graphs, where $N$ is the number of nodes and $M$ is the number of edges. For large urban networks---which routinely contain hundreds of thousands of nodes---these computations become prohibitively expensive, particularly when analysis must be repeated across multiple distance thresholds or network configurations.

Sampling-based approximation offers a natural solution: rather than computing exact centrality from all source nodes, we compute from a random subset and scale the results accordingly. This approach can dramatically reduce computation time while preserving the essential ranking information that practitioners need. The critical question is: \emph{how much sampling is enough?}

This paper presents a principled answer to that question. We introduce a simple, empirically-validated model for determining the appropriate sampling probability:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:model}
  \effn = \max\left( \kProp \cdot \sqrt{r}, \minEffN \right), \quad p = \min\left(1, \frac{\effn}{r}\right)
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the network reach (mean number of nodes within the analysis distance), $\effn$ is the effective sample size, $p$ is the sampling probability, $k = \kProp$ is a fitted proportional constant, and $\minEffN$ is a minimum floor ensuring reliability at low reach.

The model has two regimes. When reach exceeds $\crossoverReach$, the proportional term dominates: the required effective sample size is $\kProp \cdot \sqrt{r}$, yielding sampling probability $p = \kProp / \sqrt{r}$ that decreases as networks grow larger. When reach is below $\crossoverReach$, the floor dominates: we require an effective sample size of at least $\minEffN$ to ensure statistical reliability.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A practical sampling model}: We derive and fit a two-parameter model that guarantees ranking accuracy $\rho \geq \targetRho$ across diverse network topologies and analysis distances. The model uses proportional scaling ($k \cdot \sqrt{r}$) with a minimum floor ($\minEffN$) to handle low-reach edge cases.

  \item \textbf{Real-world validation}: We validate the model on a \glaNnodes{}-node Greater London street network at distances from 5km to 20km, achieving speedups of \glaFiveKmSpeedup{}--\glaTwentyKmSpeedup{}$\times$ while maintaining $\rho > \glaMinRho$.

  \item \textbf{Open-source implementation}: The model is implemented in the \cityseer{} Python library, enabling automatic per-distance sampling calibration with a single parameter.
\end{enumerate}
