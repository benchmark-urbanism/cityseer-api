% Section 1: Introduction
% =============================================================================

Urban morphological analysis makes use of localised network centrality (typically variants of closeness or betweenness computed within distance thresholds) to reveal patterns of accessibility and flow in street networks. Because these metrics are typically evaluated at multiple scales, from pedestrian ($\sim$500\,m) to metropolitan ($\sim$20\,km), the computational cost compounds: each distance threshold requires a shortest-path computation from every node, and the reachable subgraph grows with the threshold. For a network of \glaNnodes{} nodes, a single multi-scale centrality analysis can take hours.

Sampling-based approximation, in which shortest paths are computed from a random subset of source nodes and the results rescaled, can reduce this cost substantially. A body of theoretical work provides formal sample complexity bounds for global centrality on general graphs, expressed in terms of total node count, graph diameter, VC-dimension, or estimator variance \citep{Eppstein2004, Bader2007, Riondato2016, Borassi2019, Pellegrina2023}. These bounds guarantee additive error $\varepsilon$, which is valuable for applications requiring absolute accuracy but costlier than necessary for applications that need only rank preservation. More fundamentally, because these bounds target global centrality without distance cutoffs, they do not directly account for the distance-dependent structure of localised analysis or calibrate sampling separately for each distance threshold. Two questions therefore remain open for localised centrality on street networks: \emph{how many samples are enough to preserve the ranking of nodes?} and \emph{do the resulting sample sizes also provide meaningful additive error guarantees?}

We address both questions. Using a variance-scaling argument for source-sampled summation estimators motivated by the H\'{a}jek estimator \citep{Hajek1971}, we hypothesise that the effective sample size required for rank stability (Spearman $\rho \geq \targetRho$) scales as $\sqrt{r}$, where $r$ is the mean number of nodes reachable within the analysis distance. This yields a simple two-parameter adaptive model, $\effn = \max(k \cdot \sqrt{r},\; n_{\min})$, that calibrates sampling separately for each distance threshold using a single reach summary per distance. We then adapt the Eppstein--Wang source-sampling bound \citep{Eppstein2004} to the localised setting by replacing the global node count with distance-bounded reach, and empirically evaluate whether the rank-calibrated sample sizes are also consistent with a Hoeffding-style additive-error bound.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A variance-based hypothesis linking sampling theory to a $\sqrt{r}$ scaling of required effective sample size for rank stability in source-sampled, summation-based centrality measures, together with empirical validation on synthetic street-network topologies.
  \item A two-parameter adaptive model fitted on synthetic networks spanning three topologies, with formal model selection across five candidate functional forms and a bootstrap hypothesis test confirming the $\sqrt{r}$ exponent.
  \item An adaptation of the Eppstein--Wang source-sampling bound to localised centrality, with empirical evaluation showing that the rank model's recommended sample sizes satisfy the Hoeffding-style bound in over 93\% of synthetic configurations. At high reach, the two approaches become numerically consistent in the sense that rank-calibrated sample sizes correspond to an implied normalised additive error on the order of $\varepsilon \approx 0.1$ under the localised bound.
  \item Validation on the Greater London (\glaNnodes{}-node) and Greater Madrid street networks at distances from 1--20\,km, achieving Spearman $\rho > \glaMinRho$ for both closeness and betweenness centrality with speedups of \glaFiveKmSpeedup{}--\glaTwentyKmSpeedup{}$\times$.
  \item An open-source implementation in the \cityseer{} Python package.
\end{enumerate}

For clarity, throughout we use $r$ to denote the mean reach (average number of nodes reachable from a source within the distance threshold). This scalar predictor is sufficient for the per-distance calibration studied here, but it does not capture all within-distance heterogeneity (e.g., boundary effects or spatial density variation), which we discuss as a limitation.
