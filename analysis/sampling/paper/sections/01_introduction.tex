% Section 1: Introduction

Network centrality measures are fundamental tools for understanding urban street networks, revealing patterns of accessibility, connectivity, and flow that shape how cities function. Closeness centrality quantifies how effectively a location can reach all others, while betweenness centrality captures the extent to which a location lies on shortest paths between other pairs of nodes. These measures have proven invaluable for urban planning, retail location analysis, and understanding pedestrian movement patterns.

However, computing network centrality at scale presents a significant computational challenge. For large urban networks---which routinely contain hundreds of thousands of nodes---exact computation becomes prohibitively expensive, particularly when analysis must be repeated across multiple distance thresholds or network configurations.

Sampling-based approximation offers a natural solution: rather than computing exact centrality from all source nodes, we compute from a random subset and scale the results accordingly. This approach can dramatically reduce computation time while preserving the essential ranking information that practitioners need. The critical question is: \emph{how much sampling is enough?}

This paper presents a practical, empirically-validated answer to that question. We derive a two-parameter model that determines the required effective sample size $\effn$ as a function of network reach $r$---the mean number of nodes within the analysis distance. The model combines a proportional scaling term ($\effn \propto \sqrt{r}$), grounded in sampling theory, with a minimum floor that ensures reliability at low reach. The resulting sampling probability $p = \effn / r$ decreases as networks grow, delivering the largest speedups precisely where computational cost is highest.

We fit the model on synthetic networks spanning three topologies and validate it on real-world street networks in Greater London (\glaNnodes{} nodes) and Madrid, achieving Spearman $\rho > \glaMinRho$ at distances from 5--20km with speedups of \glaFiveKmSpeedup{}--\glaTwentyKmSpeedup{}$\times$. Formal model comparison across five candidate functional forms and a bootstrap hypothesis test on the scaling exponent support the proposed specification. The model is implemented in the open-source \cityseer{} Python library.
